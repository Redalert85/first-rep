## üìö Detailed Rule List

### 14th Amendment Rules

1. **Procedural Due Process Requirements**
   - Elements: Notice, opportunity to be heard, impartial decision-maker
   - Mathews v. Eldridge balancing test
   - When: Government deprivation of life, liberty, or property

2. **Substantive Due Process - Fundamental Rights**
   - Fundamental rights: enumerated + privacy rights + medical treatment
   - Strict scrutiny for fundamental rights
   - Glucksberg test: deeply rooted in history or implicit in ordered liberty
   - Note: Dobbs overruled Roe (2022)

3. **Selective Incorporation of Bill of Rights**
   - Most Bill of Rights incorporated against states via 14th Amendment
   - Exceptions: 3rd, 5th grand jury, 7th, 8th excessive fines
   - 2nd Amendment incorporated (McDonald 2010)

4. **Proving Discriminatory Intent**
   - Discriminatory intent required (not just disparate impact)
   - Arlington Heights factors for proving intent
   - Three types: facial discrimination, discriminatory application, discriminatory motive

### 11th Amendment Rules

5. **State Sovereign Immunity from Suit**
   - States immune from federal court suits
   - Exception: Ex parte Young (prospective injunctive relief against officers)
   - Exception: Congressional abrogation via 14th Amendment ¬ß 5
   - Local governments NOT immune

6. **Congressional Abrogation of State Immunity**
   - Only via 14th Amendment ¬ß 5 (not Article I)
   - Clear intent required
   - Must be congruent and proportional
   - Seminole Tribe: Commerce Clause cannot abrogate

### Commerce Clause Rules

7. **Three Categories of Regulation**
   - Category 1: Channels of interstate commerce
   - Category 2: Instrumentalities and persons/things in interstate commerce
   - Category 3: Activities substantially affecting interstate commerce
   - Economic activity can be aggregated; non-economic requires direct effect

8. **Commerce Clause Limits**
   - Cannot compel commerce (NFIB individual mandate invalid)
   - Cannot commandeer state legislatures (New York)
   - Cannot commandeer state executives (Printz)
   - Non-economic activity needs substantial interstate effect

9. **Dormant Commerce Clause**
   - States cannot discriminate against interstate commerce (strict scrutiny)
   - States cannot unduly burden interstate commerce (Pike balancing)
   - Exception: market participant
   - Exception: Congressional authorization

### Necessary and Proper Clause Rules

10. **Necessary and Proper Clause Standard**
    - Legitimate end (enumerated power)
    - Appropriate means plainly adapted to end
    - Not prohibited by Constitution
    - "Necessary" = useful/convenient (not absolutely necessary)
    - McCulloch: broad construction

11. **Limits on Necessary and Proper Clause**
    - No independent power (must couple with enumerated power)
    - Cannot commandeer states
    - Cannot exceed enumerated power scope
    - Cannot violate individual rights
    - NFIB: cannot create new powers, only implement existing

### Taxing and Spending Powers Rules

12. **Congressional Taxing Power**
    - Broad power to tax for general welfare
    - Must raise some revenue OR have revenue potential
    - Tax vs. penalty distinction
    - NFIB functional analysis: revenue to Treasury, IRS enforcement, not prohibitive

13. **Conditional Spending - Dole Test**
    - 5 factors: general welfare, unambiguous, germane, no other constitutional violation, not coercive
    - NFIB: Medicaid expansion too coercive (threatened 10%+ of state budgets)
    - Germaneness required

### Executive Power Rules

14. **Presidential Domestic Powers - Youngstown Framework**
    - Category 1: Congressional authorization = maximum power
    - Category 2: Congressional silence = twilight zone
    - Category 3: Congressional prohibition = lowest power
    - Must rely on exclusive constitutional power in Category 3

15. **Executive Power in Foreign Affairs**
    - Exclusive recognition power
    - Executive agreements (no Senate approval)
    - Commander-in-chief
    - War Powers Resolution: 48-hour notice, 60-90 day limit
    - Treaties require 2/3 Senate

16. **Limits on Executive Power**
    - No lawmaking power (line-item veto invalid)
    - Cannot impound funds
    - Executive privilege qualified (not absolute)
    - Principal officers need Senate consent
    - Limited removal power for independent agencies
